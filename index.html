Energy basics

A brief overview of sources & uses

By Kaitlyn Kelly 11:59 P.M. APRIL 6, 2015

This interactive graphic examines the major sources and uses of energy in the United States from 2001 to 2014. Looking at this research helps to provide information about sustainability and energy sources that will become dominant in the future.

ENERGY SOURCES

The major energy sources consumed in the United States are petroleum (oil), natural gas, coal, nuclear and renewable energy.


Petroleum (oil)
Oil provides 92% of the total amount of energy used for transportation, but only about 1% to generate electric power.

Natural gas
Natural gas is a relatively clean burning fossil fuel but it is made up mostly of methane which is a strong greenhouse gas. Read more

Coal
Coal used to be used greatly as a fuel to heat homes and commercial buidlings. Over the past 50 years, use of coal for this purpose has decreased to almost nothing in the United States.

Nuclear
Nuclear and coal are mainly used in one sector, electric power.

Renewable energy
There are many renewable sources of energy including hydroelectric, wind, biomass wood, biomass waste, geothermal, and solar


As shown in the graph above, the usage of coal has decreased, and the usage of natural gas and renweables (while still generating significantly less energy) has increased.

ENERGY USES

The major energy sectors are transportation, industrial, residential and commercial and electric power.

Electric power
Nuclear and coal are mainly used in one sector, electric power.




Electricity in the U.S.

4 trillion kilowatthours of electricity

were generated in the United States in 2013. Fossil fuels generate most of U.S. electricity, with coal being used for about 39% of the total electricity. Steam turbines produce the majority of electricity in the U.S. When steam turbines are powered by fossil fuels such as coal, petroleum (oil), and natural gas, the fuel is burned in a furnace that heats water in a boiler which then produces steam.

Renewable energy sources provide 13% of U.S. electricity
